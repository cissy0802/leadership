Week 08 · 2026.05.27

Lateral Influence: Leading Without Authority

Topic: Lateral Influence·4 principles
"If you want to be powerful, depend on people who depend on you." — David Bradford & Allan Cohen
This week's premise: In a large org, 60% of a tech lead manager's work isn't managing their own team — it's persuading people you can't command. Asking Platform to prioritize your unblock, asking PM to accept a tech-debt sprint, asking Security to fast-track review, asking peer teams to share dependencies. Formal authority is zero in these moments, but the ownership of the outcome is 100% yours. This week we unpack four things: (1) Influence currencies — Cohen & Bradford's core insight: it's not "good vibes" or charisma, it's the structured identification of what the other side actually wants and what you can offer in exchange; (2) Stakeholder maps — the Power × Interest matrix, sorting people into sponsor / blocker / fence-sitter and treating each with a different play; (3) Coalition sequencing — wrong order = wasted effort. The right order is inner ally → swing votes → blocker last; (4) The trust bank — influence isn't manufactured in the moment of need, it's the accumulated balance of daily deposits. By the end you should have a stakeholder map, a currency exchange plan, and a coalition sequencing plan for your most important cross-functional project.
PRINCIPLE 01

Currencies of Exchange: Influence as Structured Reciprocity Not power, not charisma — explicit trade in things people value

Cohen & BradfordReciprocityExchange
One-Line Principle
Without formal authority, the only lever you have is reciprocity — but reciprocity isn't vague goodwill, it's explicit exchange of currencies. Cohen & Bradford's core insight: everyone in an org is trading in five currency types — task (resources/support), position (visibility/recognition), inspiration (vision/mission), relationship (trust/listening), personal (sense of self-worth). 90% of influence failures aren't because the other side is "uncooperative" — they fail because you want task currency (engineers) but only offer inspiration currency ("this matters for the company"). First diagnose what currency the other side actually needs, then ask "which of my currencies do they value most?"
Source Quote
"In most organizational situations, influence is the result of an exchange — the trading of something valued for something else valued in return. The 'currencies' of exchange aren't just money or formal favors; they include task support, position-related benefits like recognition and visibility, inspiration, relationship-based goods like understanding and acceptance, and even personal feelings of self-worth and gratitude." — Allan R. Cohen & David L. Bradford · Influence Without Authority (2nd ed, 2005, Chapter 2 "The Cohen-Bradford Model")
Visual: 5 Influence Currencies · Quick Reference
Currency TypeWhat they want · what you can offer
① Inspiration Sense of mission, excellence, contribution to something larger. e.g.: frame the project as "this is how we level up our engineering bar" / "first-of-its-kind in the industry." Note: heavily inflated; low ROI when used alone.
② Task Actual resources, headcount, info, technical help, fast unblock on key dependencies. e.g.: lend an engineer to help platform write integration spec / share your team's load-test infra / review a critical RFC.
③ Position Visibility, credibility, exposure to senior leadership, promotion ammunition. e.g.: highlight their work in staff meeting / write them into a quarterly review to VP / recommend them for a tech talk.
④ Relationship Trust, being heard, being understood, being inside the inner circle. e.g.: give them an honest read on org weather / back them privately when criticized / loop them into early discussion rather than fait accompli.
⑤ Personal Sense of competence, growth, autonomy, gratitude expressed. e.g.: give them a stretching ownership opportunity / public thank-you / ask them for advice (being asked is itself a personal currency).

→ Most tech leads only use ① (inspiration) and a sliver of ⑤. The other three are where the actual leverage sits.

Scene: Asking Platform Team to Fast-Track a Feature
Q3 is starting. You need Platform to deliver an API change in the first 4 weeks, otherwise your product launch slips 6 weeks. Platform is fully booked. You have minimal history with the Platform Director.
Bad version (inspiration currency only)

Email to Platform Director: "This feature is critical to the company's Q3 OKR — please prioritize. Our product launch timing depends on it."

→ Why it fails: Platform Director gets 10 emails like this every week. "Critical to the company" is inflated inspiration currency — he can't differentiate priority. Default response: "Q3 is full, please submit a Q4 ticket."

Good version (diagnose first, then offer substantive exchange)

Step A (diagnose): Book 30 minutes with Platform's PM. Ask: "What are your team's top priorities this quarter? Where's your biggest pain point?"

PM: "We want to do X migration, but we lack a design partner to validate the API — we need a real consumer team, but no one wants to be early adopter."

Step B (offer exchange): To Platform Director:
"I have a proposal. My product team can be your design partner for the X migration:
• I'll dedicate 1 senior engineer to help write your integration spec and provide weekly feedback (task currency)
• At quarter end I'll write a case study, place it in staff meeting and quarterly write-up — VP visibility I'll channel for you (position currency)
• I'm offering myself as a reliable future design partner — when you need reciprocal priority access, we can talk (relationship currency)
In exchange, I'm asking you to unblock our API change in the first 4 weeks. I know it's an ask — but the exchange is real, not an IOU."

→ Why it works: you offered three currencies (task / position / relationship) for one task currency. Each is specific, redeemable, and matches what they actually need. The Platform Director is now evaluating a fair exchange, not being asked for a favor. Even if he can only deliver 70% (partial unblock), the relationship nets positive.

Checklist Before Asking
  • Diagnose first: what currency do they most need right now? Can't answer → don't ask. Diagnose before negotiating.
  • List 5 currencies you could offer, each made specific to an action — "I'll promote you" → "I'll write you into the staff meeting deck and skip-level write-up."
  • The trade must look visibly fair. An asymmetric ask must add long-term commitment ("when you need to ask priority next, we'll reciprocate").
  • Avoid IOU economy. Spot trade > credit. Long-term IOUs get forgotten or disputed.
  • Give them a story they can sell upward. They have to explain to their boss why priority shifted — your exchange should give them a ready narrative.
  • Record the exchange. One follow-up email confirming mutual understanding — avoids the "I never agreed" conversation 6 months later.
Common Pitfalls
  • Treating inspiration as a universal solvent. "It matters for the company" works ~30% of the time. The other 70% you need task / position / relationship currency.
  • Confusing "being friendly" with influence. Smiling requests and frowning requests both fail when there's no exchange content. Attitude is orthogonal.
  • Using position currency to coerce ("VP will ask"). Wins short-term, but tags you as a bully. Next time they dodge.
  • Offering currency you find convenient, not what they need. You give your team swag, they need engineer hours. Wrong currency ≠ deposit.
  • Forgetting their boss. They have to defend the priority shift upstream. Help them frame it upfront.
  • Making the exchange transactional to the point of obvious. "I give X, you give Y" naked enough that they feel bought. Good exchanges are structurally clear but conversationally natural.
Female Lens
Female Lens · Invisible deposits, Office housework, Glue work

Women face two asymmetric costs in currency exchange: (1) They tend to give relationship currency disproportionately (emotional support, listening, coordination), but the org evaluates contribution in task / position currency — so their "deposits" don't accrue to the official ledger; (2) They are more frequently asked to do "office housework" (take notes, organize celebrations, plan offsites) = relationship currency given one-way with no ask back. Joan Williams' empirical work: women are asked to do office housework 2–3× more often than men. Tanya Reilly in The Staff Engineer's Path calls this the "glue work" trap — necessary but underrewarded work that systematically falls to women.

Three concrete moves:

(1) Price relationship currency explicitly: "Happy to help onboard your new hires — and I'd love 30 minutes with your Director to discuss platform roadmap." Turns one-way into two-way exchange.

(2) Refuse low-status currency without attacking, per Lara Hogan's script in Resilient Management: "Great suggestion. I'm at capacity this quarter, but [X person] would be excellent for this — it's exactly the scope they're looking for." Deflection that simultaneously becomes a visibility deposit for someone else.

(3) Make deposits partially visible: women's invisible deposits won't surface on their own. Once a month write a 1-page memo to your manager listing your cross-functional contributions. Not bragging — counter-weighting the invisibility of invisible work.

This Week's Action

List your single most important cross-functional ask. Write down: (a) which currency they most need (b) three specific currencies you can offer (c) the full exchange proposal. Book a 30-minute face-to-face with them this week and run it.

PRINCIPLE 02

Stakeholder Map: Power × Interest + Archetypes Map first, engage second; engage proportionally, not democratically

MendelowPower × InterestStakeholder
One-Line Principle
Before you push anything cross-functional, drawing a stakeholder map is a 20-minute investment that returns 20 hours. Wrong mode: treat everyone equally — same update to everyone, same 1:1 frequency. Right mode: sort by Power (decision influence) × Interest (how much they care), four quadrants, each with a different cadence and depth. Mendelow's 1991 matrix is still one of the sharpest tools in a PM/TLM toolkit — simple enough to draw in 5 minutes, but every drawing reveals 1–2 stakeholders you'd otherwise miss.
Source Quote
"Stakeholders differ along two axes: the power they have to affect the outcome, and the interest they have in doing so. Investing the same effort across all stakeholders is the most common waste of influence capital. The discipline is simple but rare: map first, engage second; engage proportionally, not democratically." — Aubrey Mendelow · "Stakeholder Mapping" (Kent State, 1991); later systematized in Johnson & Scholes' Exploring Corporate Strategy (Chapter 4) as the standard business-school framework

Scene: Pushing Service Mesh Migration Across 3 Teams
You're leading a service mesh migration across 3 teams. Seven stakeholders: your Director, the VP, Platform Director A, downstream SDE team A's EM, downstream SDE team B's EM, Security Director, and a weakly-coupled Data team Director.
Bad version (no map · treat everyone equally)

You email the RFC to all 7 stakeholders and schedule an all-hands. Results:
• VP (HPHI) feels blindsided — no one prepped him, he gives -1 in the meeting
• Platform Director A (HPLI) feels overcommunicated to, starts resenting you
• Downstream SDE team A (LPHI) wasn't told the details, 4 weeks later discovers their API is broken — blocks
• Security (HPHI) wasn't looped in early, delivers -1 at the end, project slips 2 months
• Data team (LPLI) sends a flood of irrelevant questions, eating your time

→ Why it fails: treating everyone as the same stakeholder. HPHI is under-invested (surprise), HPLI is over-invested (annoyed), LPHI is neglected (becomes blocker), LPLI is engaged for no purpose (drains resources).

Good version (map first · invest by quadrant)

VP (HPHI · sponsor-to-be): Week-1 pre-wire 1:1, 30 minutes. Listen to his framing, ask which metrics he cares about. He goes from blindsided to sponsor. Biweekly 15-minute sync thereafter.

Your Director (HPHI · sponsor): Take 10 minutes of an existing weekly 1:1 slot. Align earliest.

Platform Director A (HPLI · likely coach): 1-page biweekly status email, FYI not action. Monthly 15-minute sync. Don't burden.

Downstream EMs A & B (LPHI · possible skeptics): Week-2 dedicated 30-minute walkthrough. Give them a concern channel (Slack + biweekly office hours). Their concerns go into the RFC — they become quiet supporters.

Security Director (HPHI · easily turns blocker): Loop in Week 1. Let them review architecture; write their security framework language into the RFC. Make them a co-author rather than a reviewer.

Data team (LPLI · monitor): Quarterly digest only. Reassess if project phase changes.

→ Result: a 4-week migration completes in 8 weeks with zero major blocker. Same effort, allocated by map — ROI improves 3–5×. Key insight: the map isn't drawn once. Redraw monthly — stakeholders migrate quadrants during reorgs, promotions, project phases.

Stakeholder Mapping Checklist
  • When listing stakeholders, include shadow decision-makers: the VP's Chief of Staff, the Director's trusted tech lead, the HR Business Partner — people not on the org chart but high in influence. Missing them = later ambush.
  • Annotate each with an archetype: Sponsor / Coach / Skeptic / Blocker / Fence-sitter. Different archetypes need different plays (see Card 3).
  • HPHI = at least one weekly touch; other quadrants dramatically less. Invest disproportionately in HPHI.
  • Power isn't only title: look at real decision rights, informal influence, closeness to the sponsor. A senior IC sometimes wields more power than a manager.
  • Interest includes negative interest: people whose interests your project damages are high-interest (in the negative direction) — more dangerous than fence-sitters; engage them directly.
  • Redraw monthly. The map is a living document — reorgs, promotions, project phases all shift quadrants.
  • Keep the map private. A document on the company wiki that explicitly classifies people will be screenshotted and circulated by end of day. Your craft should be invisible.
Common Pitfalls
  • Treating everyone as HPHI. Resources run out → real sponsors get diluted → key quadrant under-invested.
  • Missing the HPLI quadrant. "They don't care, so I don't need to manage them" — wrong. They suddenly intervene during reorg / budget / crisis and you're caught flat-footed.
  • Treating fence-sitters as allies. Silent ≠ supportive. They lean toward the winning side mid-project — pre-wiring is the only way to convert them.
  • Missing negative stakeholders. Whose interests does your project damage (cuts scope, increases on-call, makes their metrics ugly)? Not identifying = later ambush.
  • Drawing the map once and never updating. The Week-1 map is stale by Week 8.
  • Confusing Power with friendliness toward you. Power = influence over the decision, independent of attitude. A Director who hates the proposal is still HPHI, not LPHI.
This Week's Action

Draw a stakeholder map for your most important cross-functional project (spreadsheet is fine). For each stakeholder list Power (H/L), Interest (H/L), Archetype, and what to invest this week / this month. Then ask yourself: which stakeholder did I not realize was HPHI? Whose negative interest had I not identified?

PRINCIPLE 03

Coalition Sequencing: Inner Ring → Swing Votes → Blocker Last The wrong order isn't courage — it's a tactical error

Will LarsonHeath BrothersCoalition
One-Line Principle
Coalition building has a correct order; the wrong order wastes effort and exposes you. Wrong mode: go straight to the blocker, try to convince them — they're maximally defensive, you have no allies, you're a lone soldier in hostile territory. Right mode: inner ring first (1–2 already-aligned peers) → coaches (senior voices who give feedback without direct stake) → swing votes (fence-sitters) → public reveal → blocker last. By the final step, the blocker sees not your lone proposal but one already endorsed by 5+ peers. They either join or stand alone. This is Saul Alinsky's organizing principle, Heath Brothers' "find the bright spots" in Switch, and Will Larson's explicit advice in Staff Engineer for pushing proposals.
Source Quote
"The best way to convince a skeptic is rarely to talk to them first. Talk to the willing first — they sharpen your argument, become co-authors, and give you the social proof to bring along the doubtful. Confronting opposition before you have a coalition isn't courage — it's a tactical error that wastes the proposal's best chance." — Will Larson · Staff Engineer: Leadership Beyond the Management Track (2021, "Working with Peers" chapter)
Visual: 5-Phase Coalition Sequence
Phase 1 · Inner Ring
1–2 already-aligned peers. Refine the idea together. Get their names on the doc — once their names are there, they're invested. At this phase the idea can change drastically; harsh critique is welcome.
↓
Phase 2 · Coaches
1–2 high-reputation voices without direct stake (a principal engineer, a retired director). Get critical feedback. They don't formally endorse but signal "this is stronger than v1" — you carry that signal into Phase 3.
↓
Phase 3 · Swing Votes
3–5 fence-sitters. 30 minutes 1:1 each. Goal is listening, not persuading — write their concerns into the doc, which makes them invested. About half become quiet supporters.
↓
Phase 4 · Public Reveal
RFC / meeting / public doc. Inner ring is on the author list, swing votes nod in comments, coach has given implicit endorsement. Public reveal is no longer a cold pitch — it's ratifying an already-formed consensus.
↓
Phase 5 · Face Blocker
Only now find the blocker. Frame: "5–8 teams have reviewed this; your perspective matters most to me. What are the 3 things you're most concerned about?" The blocker now faces a coalition, not a single person. The political cost of being the sole holdout is high. Most will move to abstain ("I have concerns but OK to proceed").

→ The classic failure mode for new managers: jumping straight to Phase 5 ("get the hard one out of the way first"). Blocker delivers an immediate -1, social signal spreads, and the proposal dies before Phase 4.

Scene: Pushing a Company-Wide Code Review Standard Across 8 Teams
You want to roll out a code review standard across 8 teams. Among the 8 EMs: 2 already do something similar, 4 neutral, 2 oppose any "process for process sake" (Director B is the biggest skeptic). You have 5 weeks.
Bad version (go straight to the biggest blocker)

Week 1 you walk into Director B's office. 30-minute pitch. He gives -1 — he's always opposed "process for process sake." Word spreads. Other EMs see "B said no," go into wait-and-see. Week 3 you call an all-hands; 6 people sit silently. Proposal dies.

→ Why it fails: ① B saw raw idea + lone author — easy to reject; ② the social signal you broadcast to the org is "B rejected this" — herd behavior follows; ③ you walked alone into hostile territory — worst possible odds.

Good version (follow the 5-phase sequence)

Week 1 (Inner Ring): Find 2 staff engineers already doing something similar. 1:1: "I want to push X — help me see what's weak." Refine together. Both become co-authors.

Week 2 (Coaches): Find 1 principal engineer with experience leading similar initiatives. 45 minutes of critical feedback. He flags 3 gaps (missing metrics, vague rollout plan, no reviewer training). You patch them. He doesn't formally endorse but quietly says "much stronger than v1" — you can quote that later.

Week 3 (Swing Votes): Four neutral EMs, 30 minutes each. You're not asking "do you support" — you're asking "if this had to ship, what would you most worry about?" All 4 concerns go into a v2 doc's "addressed concerns" section. Of 4, three become quiet supporters ("my concerns were heard"), one stays neutral.

Week 4 (Public Reveal): RFC published. 2 co-authors as visible authors. 3 swing supporters nod in comments: "this addresses my concern from our discussion." Principal engineer publicly comments "v2 stronger than I expected." Social signal becomes "5 people endorse + your proposal."

Week 5 (Face Blocker): Now find Director B. Frame: "6 teams have reviewed this; your perspective matters most — your experience pushing X is most relevant. What are your 3 biggest concerns?" B now faces a 6 vs 1 coalition — sole opposition cost is high. He raises 2 concerns (reviewer fatigue, process overhead), you incorporate. He ends with abstain: "I have concerns but OK to proceed." Proposal passes.

→ Same content, different order, completely different result. Bad version is assault-the-walls; good version is encircle-then-negotiate — giving the blocker a face-saving abstain exit, far more realistic than asking for yes.

Coalition Building Checklist
  • First conversation must be inner ring, not blocker. Refining happens with allies, not in hostile territory.
  • Inner ring members co-author. Their names on the doc = their reputation tied = they're invested.
  • Swing-vote 1:1s are about listening, not persuading. Their concerns become sections of your doc. They're now invested. Conversion via ask-and-incorporate is 10× hard sell.
  • Blocker last. By the time you face them, they face a coalition, not you alone.
  • Give the blocker a face-saving exit. "I have concerns but OK to proceed" is more realistic than yes — don't force endorsement.
  • At every phase, ask: did I really finish the previous phase? Skipping phases → high failure rate.
  • Don't broadcast "lobbying" signals. Pre-wiring is listening to others' thinking, not vote-collecting. If you turn it into vote-counting, you'll be tagged as a political operator.
  • Inner ring = 1–2 people, not more. 5 co-authors = decision by committee = no owner = no momentum.
Common Pitfalls
  • Mistaking "sequencing" for manipulation. It's actually the opposite — sequencing reduces cognitive load for each stakeholder, gives each room to think, and respects them more than a cold all-hands pitch.
  • Inner ring too big. 5 co-authors = diluted ownership = idea drift. 1–2 tight allies is enough.
  • Skipping the Coaches phase. Coach is your idea-quality detector — without one, your proposal ships with 30% garbage that the blocker will spot immediately.
  • Trying to hard-sell during Swing Votes. Reverse it. Ask questions, listen, incorporate — conversion is 10× higher than sales pitch.
  • Inner ring as echo chamber. Deliberately include 1 person in the inner ring who can criticize you — otherwise the proposal hasn't survived adversarial testing before going public.
  • Trying to convert the blocker to yes in Phase 5. Too ambitious. Blocker → abstain is a win. Blocker → enthusiastic supporter usually takes 6+ months of cross-project trust building.
  • No spacing between phases. Phase 1 in the morning, Phase 4 all-hands in the afternoon — you burn the refinement value. Each phase needs 3–5 days minimum.
Female Lens
Female Lens · Coalition ROI vs Backlash Risk

Coalition building is double-edged for women. ROI is high: a lone-dissent woman gets labeled "abrasive"; a coalition reframes dissent from "personal complaint" to "collective judgment" — depersonalizing and protecting you. Risk is also high: women building coalitions get labeled "cliquish" or "political," while men doing the same are "well-networked." Same behavior, different label — Williams & Dempsey document this in What Works for Women at Work.

Three concrete moves:

(1) Cross-gender allies first. An all-women coalition gets discounted in male-dominated orgs. A mixed coalition is harder to dismiss. Ensure your inner ring has at least 1 male ally.

(2) Frame as "we built this," not "I led this." Women explicitly highlighting collective ownership reduces backlash; men need individual attribution instead. Ironic but empirical.

(3) Document contributions. The amplification protocol applies in coalition building too — Will Larson explicitly notes in Staff Engineer that women staff engineers' work often gets re-attributed to male collaborators. Written record is your only defense. After each phase, send a short follow-up email thanking by name: "Thanks [name] for sharpening X" — both a deposit and an attribution anchor.

This Week's Action

For your most important cross-functional push, list: (a) Inner Ring 1–2 people (b) Coach 1 person (c) Swing votes 3–5 people (d) Blocker 1–2 people. This week, complete the Inner Ring first conversation — resist the urge to jump ahead to the blocker.

PRINCIPLE 04

The Trust Bank: Influence Is Daily Deposits, Not Crisis Currency By the time you need a favor, it's too late to build the relationship

CialdiniAdam GrantReciprocity
One-Line Principle
Cross-functional influence isn't manufactured in the moment of need — it's the accumulated balance of daily deposits. By the time you urgently need Platform to prioritize you, you're already late; successful cross-functional leaders deposited 6 months ago, when they needed nothing. This is the long-form of Cialdini's reciprocity. Adam Grant in Give and Take distinguishes three types: Taker (only takes, short-term wins, long-term isolation), Matcher (transactional reciprocity, safe but capped), Giver (gives freely — but distinguish self-sacrificing givers from strategic "otherish" givers). Grant's counterintuitive finding: long-term success belongs to strategic Givers — they give a lot, but they give well. Uncalibrated giving = exploitation + burnout.
Source Quote
"The rule of reciprocation, in its most fundamental form, requires that one person try to repay, in kind, what another person has provided. By virtue of the reciprocity rule, we are obligated to the future repayment of favors, gifts, invitations, and the like. The web of indebtedness is the very fabric of human society." — Robert Cialdini · Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion (Chapter 2 "Reciprocation")
"Successful givers aren't just more other-oriented than their peers; they're also more self-interested. Successful givers, it turns out, are just as ambitious as takers and matchers... they have to be 'otherish' — concerned about benefiting others, but also keeping their own interests in the rearview mirror." — Adam Grant · Give and Take (2013, Chapter 6 "The Scrooge Shift")
Visual: Trust Bank · Deposits & Withdrawals Ledger

Deposits · Daily Accumulation

  • 5-minute favor: refer a peer's candidate ("send the resume, I'll chat in 30")
  • Visibility deposit: highlight others' work in staff meeting ("X's team's migration is a great example of...")
  • Quiet support: review a peer's doc without asking for credit
  • Knowledge deposit: turn your team's learning into a 1-page memo shared org-wide
  • Information deposit: give an honest read on org weather / reorg rumors (when you know)
  • Personal acknowledgment: when they hit a milestone, send a thank-you note cc'd to their manager
  • Ask for advice: being asked is itself a personal-currency deposit — reduces their face cost

Withdrawals · In Critical Moments

  • Urgent resource ask: need their team to prioritize next quarter
  • Abnormal priority shift: Platform interrupts a sprint to unblock you
  • Information ask: "privately, is X team being reorged? how do we prep?"
  • Cover in a meeting: "back me up on Y when it comes up"
  • Introduction: ask them to intro you to senior leadership / refer a candidate
  • Voice for you when absent: they advocate your direction in rooms you're not in
  • Honest critique: "tell me my blind spot" — yes, this is also a withdrawal

→ Healthy account ratio: 5–7 deposits : 1 withdrawal per counterparty. Below 3:1 = you're overdrawing.

Scene: One Year of Deposits vs Zero-Balance Withdrawal
Next quarter you need a favor from the Platform Director — interrupt their sprint to unblock you. Two histories:
Bad version (zero balance · cold withdrawal)

Past 12 months you've had near-zero interaction with the Platform Director. Out of nowhere: "Director, we need your team to prioritize Project X next quarter. Can you help?"

Him: "We're fully booked in Q3, sorry."
You: "VP signed off."
Him: "Then VP can tell me directly."

→ Why it fails: at zero balance, withdrawal not only fails — it overdraws. He flags you as "only asks, never gives." Future asks get discounted. You've now gone negative.

Good version (12 months of compounded deposits)

M1: Platform is hiring a staff engineer. You proactively refer a candidate — 5-minute favor, low cost, he remembers.

M3: They built an internal tool. You highlight it in staff meeting: "X's team's tool saved my team 2 sprints." (visibility deposit)

M5: Q2 reorg rumors. You privately give him an honest read: "I'm hearing VP is re-examining platform investment — might be worth prepping the ROI brief early." (information deposit)

M7: You review one of their RFCs and offer 3 substantive comments. No credit asked. (quiet support)

M9: They had an outage and ran a postmortem. You publicly note: "Their response was disciplined; I'll share the learnings with my team." (normalize blameless culture · personal currency)

M12 (withdrawal): "Director, next quarter we need your team to prioritize Project X. Here's a 1-page brief, impact data, and what we can reciprocate. I know it's an ask — so I'm coming to you 3 weeks ahead."

Him: "Let me see what we can do. Send the brief, I'll discuss with my staff."

→ Same ask, different balance, different outcome. Even if he can only deliver 70% — the relationship still nets positive, and you can withdraw again next time. Key: none of those 12 months of deposits was made "for this withdrawal." Each was substantive and standalone. That's the craft of strategic giving.

Trust Bank Checklist
  • Monthly review: how many cross-functional deposits did I make this month? Less than 3 = warning sign.
  • Give 5-minute favors generously, 5-hour favors carefully. Grant's rule — the former has high ROI with no burnout risk; the latter must be strategic.
  • Deposits must be substantive, not performative. A blanket "great job!" is noise. Specific reference to their work is a deposit.
  • Don't keep score obviously. Matchers' transactional vibe is detectable — strategic givers' craft is "ledger clear internally, behavior natural externally."
  • When withdrawing, explicitly acknowledge balance. "I know I'm asking a lot, here's what I can reciprocate" earns 5× the trust of "you should help me."
  • Deposit across levels: peers, reports, leadership, cross-org senior. Today's report is tomorrow's peer — not depositing down the hierarchy is a common mistake.
  • Burnout signal: you've been giving for 3 months and no one's reciprocated = recalibrate or exit the relationship. Selfless givers go bankrupt.
Common Pitfalls
  • Quid pro quo too explicit. "I help you X, you help me Y" naked enough to feel bought — trust drops, not rises.
  • Withdrawing too early. Asking before depositing — they default to "you're a taker." On any new cross-functional relationship, deposit at least 3 times before withdrawing.
  • Withdrawing too frequently. Asking again before the balance has recovered — they start avoiding your calendar invites.
  • Selfless giver trap. Grant repeatedly warns: pure altruism puts givers at the bottom of the success ladder — giving until burnout loses both yourself and the total volume you can give long-term. "Otherish" is sustainable.
  • Visible-only deposits. Beyond loud praise in staff meetings, quiet support (background review, no credit asked) actually builds higher trust ROI. Do both.
  • Depositing with implicit expectation of specific reciprocation. True deposits expect no specific return — return comes from the balance, not the transaction. The more you expect specific returns, the closer you are to matcher than giver.
  • Burning visible deposits on strangers. When resources are scarce, invest in established trust — cold networks are long-tail, not cap table.
Female Lens
Female Lens · Invisible Deposits, Likability Tax on Withdrawals

Women face asymmetric costs in the deposit/withdrawal economy: (1) "Invisible deposits" don't accrue to the official ledger — women do more invisible support (emotional support, coordination, glue work), but orgs evaluate by visible output. Tanya Reilly explicitly addresses the "glue work" trap in The Staff Engineer's Path: necessary but underrewarded work systematically falls to women; (2) Likability cost on withdrawal is higher — the same ask "I need X next quarter" reads as "demanding" from a woman and "assertive" from a man. Same phrasing, different cost.

Three concrete moves:

(1) Deposits must include a visible component. e.g., amplify peers in staff meeting, give credit in written postmortems, list cross-functional contributions in quarterly memos. Not bragging — counter-weighting the invisibility of invisible work.

(2) Frame withdrawals around impact, not need. "This unblocks X for the customer/company" triggers less likability tax than "I need it." Reframe the ask from personal need to org need.

(3) Refuse glue work without backlash. Lara Hogan's script: "Great suggestion. I'm at capacity, but [X] would be excellent for this scope — it's exactly the opportunity they're looking for." Refusal that simultaneously becomes a visibility deposit for someone else. Two birds, one stone.

Open Questions

Is structuring human interaction as "currency exchange" dehumanizing? Does it turn colleagues into means?
This is the most common objection to the Cohen & Bradford model — it reduces people to "what they want." But examined closely, the currency frame is actually more respectful. The default mode ("I'm asking you because we're colleagues") implicitly treats the other as obligated to pay your costs — you presuppose their obligation. The currency model reverses this: it acknowledges they owe you nothing, they have their own goals, pressures, scarce resources; if you want their energy, you should substantively offer something they value rather than trade on goodwill. This is Kant's "treat humans as ends not means" operationalized — taking their utility function seriously rather than assuming they should prioritize yours. What's actually dehumanizing is the inspiration-only mode: "this is for the company" treats them as ideology-bearers who should self-sacrifice. So the real boundary is not "use currency thinking or not" — it's (a) whether the currency you offer is what they actually need vs what's convenient for you; (b) whether you're transparent about the thinking ("I want to make this a fair exchange because I value the relationship"); (c) whether you also give without expecting return (the strategic-giver baseline). All three conditions met, the currency frame respects agency more than vague obligation does.
Long-term, who in an org has the most influence without formal authority? What are the recurring patterns?
Long-term, it's people who combine three traits: (1) Scarce but generous professional judgment — when people ask, you give a real, valuable take, not hedged corporate-speak. You become the "go-to" person; others default to thinking of you; (2) Steady deposit behavior + light withdrawal — large account, modest withdrawals. The phenotype is "everyone owes them but no one feels burdened" — counterparties are happy to reciprocate because they remember being helped many times; (3) Structured information broker — you span multiple silos, translating intent between A team and B team, surfacing misunderstandings, flagging conflicts early. Adam Grant covers this in Originals; Tanya Reilly's glue work concept captures the same dynamic — they're invisible-but-critical connective tissue. Watch for an anti-pattern: many people equate "influence" with "large network." It's actually a high-quality small network. Larson cites data in Staff Engineer: effective network for staff+ engineers is typically 15–30 people, not 200. Depth of deposit > breadth of networking. A counterintuitive final note: the most influential people aren't always the most visible — they let visible attribution go to others while holding the "trusted advisor" position. This is Grant's core distinction between "successful giver" and "performative giver."
How does the currency exchange model adapt to East Asian / Chinese big-company culture? Does guanxi cover everything?
Meaningful differences, not a dichotomy. East Asian high-context, face-driven, long-horizon culture shapes the form of currency exchange differently, but the underlying mechanism is the same. Concretely: (1) Relationship currency carries more weight — in Western orgs, you can push with task + position currency; in East Asian orgs, relationship currency is a prerequisite — without it, other currencies are discounted. That's guanxi — but guanxi isn't magic; it's structurally long-term deposits + mutual face investment; (2) Explicit transaction backfires — Western orgs accept "let's do an exchange" as mature; East Asian orgs read this as transactional and disrespectful. Currency still gets exchanged, but the framing must be implicit: "let's make this work together" rather than "I'll do X if you do Y"; (3) Position currency works differently — Western orgs: visibility → directly toward promotion / external recognition. East Asian orgs: visibility must be mediated through "giving face to the boss" — you don't directly highlight a peer; you make sure their boss sees their work; (4) Time horizon differs — East Asian reciprocity time horizon is in years; Western is in months. Early deposits don't pay off quickly in East Asia, but accumulate higher and more durably. Sun Tzu and Han Fei here are more directly relevant than Bezos. The "伐交" chapter of Sun Tzu and Han Fei's "Inner Storehouses" are more directly useful for East Asian lateral influence than reading Cohen & Bradford alone. But don't use "East Asian culture" as an excuse for skipping substantive exchange — Ren Zhengfei, Zhang Yiming have all publicly said the people they distrust most are those who are "all guanxi, no substance." Substantive currency works cross-culturally; only phrasing and rhythm differ.
Does remote / hybrid work make "trust deposits" harder or easier? How do you deposit asynchronously?
Verdict: harder, but systematically compensable. Remote reduces incidental deposit opportunities — the "casual review of a doc," "unsolicited advice over coffee," "check-in when someone looks stressed" — these accounted for 40%+ of traditional deposits. Microsoft's 2021 internal research (New Future of Work Report) data: post-remote, cross-team collaboration networks became sparser; cross-silo ties measurably weakened — which is exactly the substrate of lateral influence. But remote also creates new deposit types: (1) Async written deposits have high ROI — a thoughtful 1-page memo shared org-wide reaches further and lasts longer than office-floor comments. Will Larson repeatedly emphasizes "write things down" as the core of distributed lateral influence; (2) Public Slack channels create more visibility than hallways — solving a peer's technical question in #general is a 5-minute deposit visible to 200 people; (3) 1:1 video calls are more intentional than hallway chats — but because they're intentional, deep deposits (like "how are you really doing?") require deliberate scheduling. Three deliberate moves: (a) Calendar "office hours" — 30 minutes weekly with open Zoom for anyone cross-team to drop in; (b) Async deposit habit — when you read an article / paper / RFC useful for a peer, DM it: "this made me think of your X work" — 5-second cost, long memory; (c) Periodic 1:1 outside team — monthly 30 minutes with 2 cross-org peers, no agenda. Sustained over 6 months, lateral influence is comparable to or higher than in-office days — but requires deliberate scaffolding to replace incidental contact.
How do you distinguish a "strategic giver / influencer" from a "manipulative networker / political operator"? They look similar from outside.
This is the core question Grant addresses in Give and Take and Originals. From outside, they look similar — both deposit, both build networks, both are reciprocity-aware. Acid tests for the distinction: (1) Are deposits unconditional at the start of relationships? Strategic givers deposit before seeing potential return (refer a candidate to a totally unfamiliar team); manipulative networkers only deposit to "useful" relationships — visible by how they treat lower-status people. Anyone treated as "useful" by a manipulative networker who later loses status will be dropped immediately; (2) Do they explicitly acknowledge the balance when withdrawing? Strategic givers say "I know I'm asking a lot here"; manipulative networkers wrap their withdrawal as "for the team/company" to avoid admitting they're asking; (3) Do they say uncomfortable truths against their own interest? Strategic givers will honestly disagree even when reputationally costly (Card 1's substantive critique); manipulative networkers always align with current power — this is the sharpest test, because manipulators can't fake long-term honest dissent; (4) What's the ratio of invisible deposits? Pure visible deposits (public highlights, staff meeting acknowledgments) could be performative. Strategic givers' ledgers have 30–50% invisible deposits (background reviews, no-credit help) — this can't be faked, because the counterparty knows you got nothing and still helped. A complementary observation: recognizing the distinction is itself a scarce skill — many senior leaders hover on the borderline themselves, so they can't reliably distinguish in others. That's how some manipulators rise sustainably — until a self-aware giver systematically calls them out. Robert Greene's 48 Laws of Power and Cunning of History give tools from the manipulator's perspective; Grant gives tools from the long-term-integrity perspective. Choosing which toolkit reflects who you want to be, and shapes the quality of the coalition you can build 5–10 years out.
