Lessons from War & Sport: Four Borrowed Blades — Leave the Blood Behind

Topic: Lessons from War & Sport·4 principles
"Eyes on, hands off." Mission command, the coaching philosophy, team cohesion, decisions under pressure — four battle-tested tools, translated into moves you can actually run inside a big company.
This week's thesis: The military and competitive sport are the two fields that have studied "how to make a group win under high pressure, high uncertainty, high stakes" the longest — and they've forged a set of extremely distilled leadership tools. But copying them wholesale is dangerous: a company is not a battlefield, your reports are not soldiers, and "obedience" is not the default here. This week we pick the four blades that translate best: mission command (give intent, not steps), the coaching philosophy (you don't play; you win through others), team cohesion (separate task cohesion from social cohesion), and decisions under pressure (tempo beats optimality). For each, we mark where it works — and where it cuts you.
PRINCIPLE 01

Mission Command: Give Intent and Boundaries, Not Steps Mission Command — Intent, Not Instructions

AuftragstaktikCommander's IntentDelegation
Principle + The Masters
Two centuries ago the Prussian army learned that the battlefield changes faster than orders travel. So they issued only "what" and "why" (commander's intent + boundaries) and left "how" to the person on the ground — the one closest to the information decides, instead of relaying it up to authority. That's mission command (Auftragstaktik).
"The best way to describe my role: I tried to keep my eyes on and my hands off." — Stanley McChrystal, Team of Teams
"Don't move information to authority, move authority to the information." — L. David Marquet, Turn the Ship Around!
In Practice
Situation: An engineer comes to you — "Should this service be split into two microservices, or should we just add a cache? You tell me which."
✗ Command mode (pulls authority back to you)

"Add a cache — splitting into microservices is too heavy." You handed over a specific solution. He'll come back next time; you've become the bottleneck. And because he never understood why, the next similar call stalls too.

✓ Mission command (give intent + boundaries, keep the "how")

Intent: "What I want is P99 latency under 200ms by end of quarter, without adding more than one person's worth of ops burden."

Boundaries: "Two constraints — no new database, and the change has to be canary-able and reversible."

Hand over the how: "Inside those lines, you judge. Come back to me Marquet-style: 'I intend to add a cache, because…' — state your reasoning, and as long as it's within the lines, I nod."

Checklist: Order, or Intent?
  • Did I state the outcome and the why (intent), or just an action?
  • Did I draw hard boundaries (budget, time, untouchables) so he can move freely inside them?
  • Am I making him "ask for approval," or having him state a decision with "I intend to… because…"?
  • If his approach differs from mine but meets the intent equally well, can I resist overriding it?
  • If it fails, will I ask "how did you judge it?" rather than "why didn't you listen to me?"
Pitfalls + A Note for Women Leaders
  • Fake delegation. You say "you decide," then overturn it the moment it differs — more corrosive to trust than a direct order.
  • Boundaries without intent. "Don't overspend, don't slip" are constraints, not direction. Without a why to fight for, he just plays it safe.
  • Intent so detailed it's a disguised order. "The outcome is: add a Redis cache" — that's a how, not a what.
A Note for Women Leaders When a woman delegates, reports (especially senior men) sometimes read it as "she has no view of her own, so she's making me decide." The counter is the clarity of the intent: replace "figure it out" with "the outcome I want is X, the constraints are Y, you know the path better than I do — you own it." One sentence carries both clear ownership of direction and real delegation. A crisp intent is itself proof of authority.
PRINCIPLE 02

The Coaching Philosophy: You Don't Play — Others Score for You The Coaching Philosophy — You Don't Play

Bill CampbellJohn WoodenTeach
Principle + The Masters
The best coach never takes a shot. His entire value is in making the people on the court play better — the same identity shift as a tech leader moving from "I write the best code" to "my team writes the best code." A coach's core move isn't commanding; it's teaching: correcting without breeding resentment, until the person has actually learned.
"Coaching is no longer a specialty; you cannot be a good manager without being a good coach." — Schmidt, Rosenberg & Eagle, Trillion Dollar Coach (on Bill Campbell)
"You have not taught until they have learned." — John Wooden (10× NCAA champion coach)
In Practice
Situation: An engineer shipped without monitoring, causing a small incident. In the retro, how do you open?
✗ Referee mode (judge + wound)

"How could you miss something this basic? Run the checklist before you ship next time." He remembers the shame, learns no judgment, and next time just hides more.

✓ Coach mode (Wooden: "correct without resentment")

"Facts first, no judgment: this ship missed monitoring. Let's reverse-engineer it together — what did the checklist in your head look like before you shipped?"

(After listening) "So monitoring wasn't in your default checklist. That's not a memory problem — it's a checklist that needs upgrading. How would you change it so next time a gap like this is caught by a mechanism, not by someone remembering?"

"You lead writing that improvement, and present it to the whole team next retro — turn your lesson into a team asset."

Checklist: Teaching, or Just Commanding?
  • Did I hand over an answer, or ask a question that let him find the answer himself?
  • Did my correction target behavior and mechanism, or the person ("you're just careless")?
  • Did I confirm he actually learned (can teach it to others), rather than just nodded?
  • Did I turn this failure into personal punishment, or a shared team lesson?
  • Will my correction breed more resentment than growth? (Wooden's red line)
Pitfalls + A Note for Women Leaders
  • Treating coaching as "always rescuing." A good coach points from the sideline during timeouts, not by taking the court. The moment you step in and write the code, you stop teaching.
  • Only praise or only correction. Bill Campbell's way was "see the person sincerely first, then give the hard feedback" — the relationship is what lets correction land.
  • Taught without verifying learning. Wooden's bar is hard: if he hasn't learned, you haven't taught. Don't console yourself with "but I told him."
A Note for Women Leaders Bill-Campbell-style "caring coaching" is double-edged for women leaders: care is both expected and undervalued. Pour real energy into coaching and you risk being filed as "nice but not strategic enough." The counter: make the output visible — "the three people I coached each independently took over a module this half" — use others' growth as your leadership record, don't let it disappear into invisible "emotional labor."
PRINCIPLE 03

Team Cohesion: Tell "Winning Together" from "Grilling Together" Team Cohesion — Task vs Social

Leaders Eat LastTask CohesionOrder of Sacrifice
Principle + The Masters
Military research long ago split cohesion in two: task cohesion (trust in the shared goal and in each other's competence) and social cohesion (whether you enjoy hanging out). What actually predicts fighting power is task cohesion — too much social cohesion can even drag performance down (everyone fears hurting feelings and stops telling the truth). Cohesion isn't bought with team-building games; it's earned by the leader's order of bearing risk and sacrifice.
"Leaders are the ones willing to give up something of their own for us... When it matters, leaders choose to eat last." — Simon Sinek, Leaders Eat Last (from the Marine Corps tradition of officers eating last)
Two Kinds of Cohesion: Don't Prescribe the Wrong Cure
In Practice
Situation: The team has been grinding overtime toward a release and morale is dropping. You want to "boost cohesion."
✗ Buy social cohesion only

"Friday team outing — escape room plus dinner!" Short-term fun, but the root causes (an unreasonable schedule, invisible purpose) go untouched. They come back to the same meat grinder.

✓ Build task cohesion first + leader sacrifices first

Order of sacrifice (Leaders Eat Last): "This week I cut my own two meetings to sit in and clear bugs with you; I'll write the status report so you can stay focused." You give up your own thing first.

Rebuild task cohesion: "Let's align — why is this release worth this push? When it's done I commit two comp days for the whole team. Whatever's riskiest, we look at together — nobody carries it alone."

(After the release) then celebrate. Celebration is the glue that follows winning together — not a substitute for it.

Checklist: Which Cohesion Are You Building?
  • Under pressure, do I give up my own things first (time, credit, comfort), or demand from the team first?
  • Beneath the surface "harmony," are there truths no one dares say? (the sign of too much social cohesion)
  • Am I investing in task cohesion (clear goals, trust in each other's skill), or just buying social cohesion (dinners)?
  • Does celebration come after winning together, or get used as a painkiller for morale?
Pitfalls + A Note for Women Leaders
  • Mistaking team-building for cohesion. An escape room won't buy "I trust you to carry this module." Task cohesion only grows in real collaboration.
  • Chasing everyone-likes-everyone. The goal is mutual trust in competence plus candid conflict, not seamless friendship.
  • "Eating last" as theater. Sacrifice has to happen at the real-cost, defining moments; staged versions convince no one.
A Note for Women Leaders Women leaders are often defaulted into "team emotional caretaker" and slide into the "team mom" role — over-investing in social cohesion (remembering birthdays, mediating feelings) while their strategic contribution gets undervalued. Shifting weight toward task cohesion protects you more: clear goals, public trust in members' skill, a candid conflict culture — a stronger source of fighting power, and it gets your leadership read as "wins with the team," not "she's warm."
PRINCIPLE 04

Decisions Under Pressure: Tempo Beats Optimality Decisions Under Pressure — Tempo Beats Perfection

OODA40-70 RuleRecognition-Primed
Principle + The Masters
Under pressure (an incident, a crisis, a clutch play), the winner isn't the one who computes the optimum — it's the one whose loop spins faster than the opponent's. Fighter pilot John Boyd's OODA loop — Observe, Orient, Decide, Act — isn't about perfecting each step; it's about using faster tempo to spin the opponent into chaos. Experts don't decide by comparing options either; they recognize a pattern and pick a "good-enough" answer.
"Use the formula P = 40 to 70... Don't act if you have less than 40% of the information; don't wait until you have more than 70% — by then it's too late." — Colin Powell, It Worked for Me (Powell's leadership rules)
The OODA Loop: Spin Faster Than the Opponent
In Practice
Situation: A major production incident. You're the incident commander, information is only at 50%, and the team waits for your call.
✗ Chase the optimum (stuck in analysis paralysis)

"Hold on — let's fully nail the root cause before we decide whether to roll back." Information is rising, but so are the losses. By the time you're 100% sure, the users are gone.

✓ Seize tempo (40-70 rule + OODA)

Stop the bleeding with a good-enough move: "Info's at 50% — that's enough. Roll back to the last stable version to stop the bleeding — it's reversible, cost is bounded. Investigate root cause in parallel."

Iterate fast: "We sync the situation every 15 minutes and adjust the call as new info lands. Don't aim to get it right in one shot — aim to spin faster than the incident."

Orient on experience: "Last time this kind of latency spike was a maxed-out connection pool — check that hypothesis first, switch instantly if wrong." (recognition-primed decision)

Checklist: Pressure-Decision Self-Check
  • Is my information at 40%? (below it, don't guess) At 70%? (at it, stop waiting)
  • Is the move I'm making reversible? If reversible, act fast and watch; only the irreversible earns more waiting.
  • Am I chasing "right in one shot," or "spinning faster than the problem," allowing correction as I go?
  • Did I set a sync cadence (re-run OODA every N minutes)?
  • After the crisis, do I review the decision process (was the call sound given the info at the time), not just the outcome?
Pitfalls + A Note for Women Leaders
  • Mistaking "decisive" for "reckless." The floor of the 40-70 rule is 40% — speed below it is gambling, not decisiveness.
  • Treating reversible and irreversible alike. Applying play-it-safe caution to a reversible move is the biggest invisible waste.
  • Reviewing only by outcome. A good decision can have a bad outcome. Reward only outcomes and the team learns to gamble, not to judge.
A Note for Women Leaders In fast, high-pressure calls, women leaders face a sharp double standard: the same decisive move at 50% info is read as "gutsy" from a man and possibly "reckless / emotional" from a woman; yet waiting for more info gets tagged "hesitant / not leader enough." A pragmatic counter: make the decision logic explicit — "info's at 50%, this is a reversible move, so we act now and watch." Let the room see you're running a method, not an impulse, and use transparency to offset attribution bias.

Deeper Questions

Military metaphors carry undertones of "obedience," "sacrifice," and "enemy" — does importing them quietly smuggle antagonism and hierarchy into a company?
This is the side effect to watch most. Overuse military metaphors and people start treating colleagues and other departments as "enemies," obedience as virtue, dissent as "shaky morale." Borrow the form, drop the soul: mission command wants the "delegation + intent" structure, not a culture of absolute subordinate obedience. Self-check: when you cite a military concept, ask whether you're borrowing a collaboration mechanism or a domination relationship — the former is usable, throw out the latter. Your real opponent is in the market, not across the table.
"Leaders eat last" is hard to live in real work — sacrifice too much and you collapse first. Where's the line?
Sinek's metaphor is often misread as "leaders must sacrifice infinitely," which leads straight to burnout. The accurate reading is order, not quantity: at key moments you bear the risk first and enjoy the reward later — not empty yourself every minute. At the defining moments (team under pressure, someone about to take the blame, credit to be allocated) you step up and give way; in the everyday, you protect your own energy and boundaries, or "eat last" becomes "collapse first" and the team loses its anchor. Sacrifice is a lever — use it on the fulcrum, don't spread it evenly across every day.
Does OODA's "seize tempo" clash with deliberate strategy? When to go fast, when slow?
The two don't conflict — they answer different questions. The dividing lines are reversibility and time pressure: high pressure + reversible (stopping the bleeding in an incident, grabbing a fleeting window) → seize tempo with OODA, good-enough first, fix as you go; low pressure + irreversible (org restructuring, core tech choices, hiring a key person) → slow down, worth pushing info to 70% or higher. Using "seize tempo" on a big irreversible call is recklessness; using "deliberation" on a small reversible one is waste. Bezos's Type 1 (one-way door) vs Type 2 (two-way door) is the same yardstick — judge the door type first, then set the speed.
