Remote & Hybrid, Deeper: Manage the People You Can't See With Systems, Not Peripheral Vision

Topic: Remote & Hybrid, Deeper·4 principles
"If you can't let your employees work from home out of fear they'll slack off... you're a babysitter, not a manager." — Jason Fried & DHH
The premise: you lead a distributed team across time zones — some in the office, some fully remote, some in two days a week. Manage it with the office instinct of "I can see who's busy when I look up," and you hit three traps at once: mistaking online status for output, mistaking the people near you for the reliable ones, and mistaking the quiet ones for gone. The real difficulty of hybrid isn't the tooling — it's that you've lost the cheap information channel of peripheral vision without replacing it with a fairer system. This issue's four principles — async-first, trust vs surveillance, hybrid fairness (fighting proximity bias), and digital presence — help you turn a team that can't see each other into one that's clearer-headed than a team sharing one room.
PRINCIPLE 01

Async-First: Change the Default From "Sync Up" to "Write It Down" Async-First — Make the Written Word the Default

asyncdocs-firsttime zones
Principle + Master's Words
On a cross-time-zone team, "let's hop on a call to sync" is the most expensive move — it demands everyone be present at once. Async-first isn't about fewer meetings; it's about flipping the default: assume a thing can be solved by writing it clearly, and only escalate to a meeting what genuinely needs live back-and-forth. Meetings drop from "first choice" to "async fallback."
"The office during the day has become the last place people want to be when they really want to get work done." — Jason Fried & David Heinemeier Hansson, Remote: Office Not Required (2013)
Scene: your Bangalore colleagues are only online at midnight, and you want a "quick sync"
Context: a design decision is stuck. You want a 30-minute call during your workday. Two teammates are in Bangalore — that's the middle of their night.
✗ Sync by default

"Let's all grab a call tomorrow at 10 a.m. (my time) to align." — You save yourself half an hour of writing, at the cost of two people staying up, or missing a decision about their own work. Over time, the time-zone-disadvantaged side vanishes from the key discussions.

✓ Async by default

"I wrote a one-page RFC with three options and the one I lean toward, plus the trade-offs. Please comment in the doc — especially the two of you in Bangalore, you know these constraints best."

"I'll collect input by noon my Thursday. Only if there's still a hard disagreement will we hold one short call in a window that covers everyone's waking hours." (Write first, meet second; the meeting is a fallback, not the start.)

Async-First Self-Check
  • Could my last "call" have been solved with a one-page doc plus async comments?
  • Do important decisions leave a searchable written record (RFC / decision log), or do they live only inside one call?
  • Are my meetings defaulted to "my daytime," permanently offloading the time-zone cost onto the same people?
  • Can a new hire ramp up by reading docs, without needing to catch someone to sync?
Common Mistakes
  • Treating async as "never interrupt me." Async is the default, not dogma; in a live incident or an emotional hard conversation, sync immediately — dragging it into a doc thread only makes it worse.
  • Writing sloppily and dumping it on others. Async front-loads its cost onto "write it clearly." A vague paragraph that triggers five rounds of follow-up is slower than a meeting.
This Week's Exercise + Reflection
Exercise: take one recurring meeting you'd normally hold and replace it with a one-page async doc plus a 24-hour comment window. Only add a short call if a real disagreement remains after input closes.
Reflection: is anyone on my team systematically excluded from important discussions simply because they're "not in my daytime"?
PRINCIPLE 02

Trust vs Surveillance: Manage the Deliverable, Not the Green Dot Trust vs Surveillance — Manage Output, Not Green Dots

trustoutputproductivity paranoia
Principle + Master's Words
When you can't see people, many managers fall back on proxy metrics: hours online, how fast messages get answered, how long the green dot glows. But those measure the ability to perform busyness, not output. The more you trade surveillance for reassurance, the more you train the team to spend energy looking like they're working. The real fix is to anchor management to clear deliverables and outcomes — say what you need, by when, and what "good" looks like — then let go.
"Leaders think their employees are less productive... but employees say they're getting more done. We call this productivity paranoia." — Microsoft Work Trend Index (2022): 85% of leaders say it's hard to be confident employees are being productive in hybrid work
Scene: a remote engineer's green dot is often grey during the day
Context: you notice a remote engineer often shows "away" on Slack during the day, and it nags at you. But his work ships on time and the quality is solid.
✗ Respond with surveillance

"I see you're often offline during the day — could you keep your status active and reply to messages ASAP?" — You get a permanently glowing green dot, and an engineer who can no longer do deep work and starts resenting you. You measured his ability to fake presence.

✓ Respond with trust

"Your delivery has been rock-solid — I have zero concern there. I only want to align on one thing: if there's an urgent blocker, how fast can I reach you?" (Shrink the ask from "always online" to "agreed responsiveness.")

"Let's set a rule: urgent goes to my phone, reply within half a day; non-urgent is async, same-day is fine. How you arrange your deep-work time otherwise is your call." (Clear SLA, autonomy returned.)

From Surveillance to Trust: A Checklist
  • Do I judge someone by deliverables and outcomes, or by proxies like hours online and reply speed?
  • Are "response expectations" spelled out (what counts as urgent, how fast a reply is due), or left to guesswork?
  • Have I installed — or wanted to install — monitoring software? If so, is my real anxiety about output, or about my own sense of control?
  • Am I rewarding "the fastest to reply" instead of "the one who thought the hard problem through"?
Common Mistakes
  • Treating "trust" as "hands off." Trust isn't disengagement; it's moving your attention from "is the process online" to "is the outcome aligned" — goals, cadence, and feedback all still matter.
  • Vague response expectations force people to stay always-on to protect themselves. Without an SLA, the cautious ones buy safety with "always reply instantly" — and they burn out first.
This Week's Exercise + Reflection
Exercise: write down a "response agreement" with the team this week — which channel for urgent vs non-urgent, and how fast a reply is due. Once it's written, stop watching anyone's green dot.
Reflection: if every presence indicator vanished tomorrow, would I still have a way to judge the team's health? If not, have I been using green dots as a substitute for actual management?
PRINCIPLE 03

Hybrid Fairness: Fight the Illusion That "Closer to Me = More Reliable" Hybrid Fairness — Fight Proximity Bias

proximity biasfairnesspromotion
Principle + Master's Words
The most insidious risk on hybrid teams is proximity bias: you unconsciously trust more, think of more often, and are more willing to promote the people you see every day. It isn't malice — it's the brain being lazy. The result is that equally capable remote colleagues get fewer opportunities and slower promotions. Hybrid fairness can't rely on your good intention to "try to be even-handed" — it needs systems that keep the bias out.
"Home workers were promoted at about half the rate of office workers, despite being more productive." — Nicholas Bloom et al., Does Working from Home Work? (Ctrip randomized trial), QJE (2015)
Scene: who gets the high-visibility opportunity
Context: a key project that gets exposure and credit needs an owner. The first name in your head is A, who chats with you at your desk daily. B — a fully remote colleague, equally qualified — barely crossed your mind.
✗ Go with the bias

"Let's just give it to A — easier to communicate anyway." — "Easier to communicate" translates to "closer to me." You keep feeding the best opportunities to whoever's physically near you; B's résumé thins out year by year, and eventually he leaves saying "there's no opportunity for me here."

✓ Use a system to block the bias

(Ask yourself first) "If A and B were both fully remote and I couldn't see either, judging on ability and past delivery alone, whom would I pick?" (Strip out physical distance, then decide.)

"I want to give this high-visibility project to B — his ability is undervalued by geography. I'll deliberately rotate high-exposure work between in-office and remote, so the visible defaults don't keep landing on the same people."

Hybrid Fairness Self-Check
  • Over the past six months, did high-visibility opportunities / promotions land disproportionately on the people who are often in the office?
  • Are meetings "worst for hybrid" — in-room folks huddled at a whiteboard while remote people are stranded in a little window? Either all in one room or all online (one screen each) — no second-class seating.
  • Does informal info (things decided in hallways or over lunch) flow back into writing so those not present can get it too?
  • Do I give remote colleagues the same 1:1 time and care as in-office ones?
Common Mistakes + A Note on Women
  • Chanting "hybrid fairness" as a slogan without changing the mechanism. Bias dissolves not through slogans but through checkable rules: opportunity rotation, info flow-back, equal-footing meetings.
  • Pressuring with "come in more and you'll get chances." That ties promotion to attendance, and it punishes exactly the people who most need flexibility.
A Note on Women Proximity bias hits women who depend more on flexible work harder: carrying more caregiving load, they're more likely to choose remote, and so more likely to be "out of sight" and left out of opportunity allocation, widening the promotion gap. Anchoring opportunity to output and ability rather than attendance-visibility is the most concrete anti-bias move you can make.
This Week's Exercise + Reflection
Exercise: list the "high-visibility opportunities" you handed out over the past six months, tagging each owner as often-in-office or remote. Look at the ratio — if it's clearly skewed, deliberately give the next one to a qualified remote person.
Reflection: how much of my gut sense of "reliable" actually comes from "I see him often" rather than "he genuinely delivers well"?
PRINCIPLE 04

Digital Presence: Writing Clearly Is the New "Being There" Digital Presence — Clear Writing Is the New Being-There

digital body languageexpressionpresence
Principle + Master's Words
In the office, a nod, a hallway hello, a tone of voice conveys trust and intent. Remote cuts all those nonverbal signals — all that's left is what you type. Whether your message is clear and considerate enough is the "you" the team experiences. Digital presence isn't sending more messages to seem present; it's making every written exchange carry the care and clarity you'd naturally radiate in person.
"In a digital world, what we write is often all people have to go on. Reading carefully is the new listening; writing clearly is the new empathy." — Erica Dhawan, Digital Body Language (2021)
Scene: an ice-cold "Got it"
Context: a remote colleague spent half a day writing a proposal and sent it to you. You're busy and dash off two words.
✗ Low digital presence

"Got it." — In the office, a glance and a smile would have supplied the tone. Through a screen, those two words read as dismissive, even displeased. She starts wondering "was it not good?" and you erode trust without realizing it.

✓ High digital presence

"Got it, thanks — chasing a deadline today, haven't read closely yet. I'll give you point-by-point feedback by Thursday morning. One thing already: the outline alone shows how clearly you thought this through."

(Spend 20 extra seconds to add: confirm receipt, give a firm time, and one sincere specific compliment — translating the office nod into text.)

Digital Presence Self-Check
  • Do my short messages read cold from being too terse? Can I add a note of tone or a timeframe?
  • Have I given a clear "when I'll get back to you," instead of leaving people waiting and guessing?
  • For important or sensitive matters, am I still forcing a text thread instead of escalating to a call/video?
  • Do I write down praise and recognition and put it where others can see it (not just keep it in my head)?
Common Mistakes
  • Reading "presence" as message frequency. Presence is quality, not quantity; flooding the channel just creates noise and dilutes trust.
  • Cramming emotionally charged matters into text. Text loses tone; the more sensitive it is, the easier it's misread — don't skimp on the five minutes a video call would take.
This Week's Exercise + Reflection
Exercise: before replying this week, pause on at least three "offhand two-word" replies and add tone, a timeframe, or one specific bit of praise. Watch how the other person's response changes.
Reflection: if my team could only know me as a leader through the words I type, would they experience "caring and clear," or "busy and cold"?

Deeper Reflection

I'm anxious too: when I can't see people, how do I know who's coasting and who's genuinely overloaded? Is "look at the deliverables" enough?
Deliverables are the baseline, but not enough — they tell you the result, not the cost. Add two cheap signals: (1) regular structured 1:1s, asking directly "where did your time go this week, what drained you most" — coasting and overload both surface in that conversation. (2) Watch cadence, not presence: whether someone's PRs / docs / discussions have a steady rhythm of output tells you far more than how long their green dot glows. Real coasters give themselves away in delivery cadence; the truly overloaded show fatigue in 1:1s or a sudden collapse in output. Build a dual channel of "outcomes + conversation," rather than retreating to watching hours online.
The company mandates "in-office at least three days." As a middle manager I'm caught in the middle: I know proximity bias is unfair, yet I have to enforce the policy. What do I do?
Don't pretend the policy doesn't exist, but you control how it lands. Three layers: (1) Separate "attendance" from "evaluation" — even with three days in, promotions and opportunities still anchor to output; state publicly and act on "I don't grade by days in office." (2) Make in-office time genuinely worth it — schedule high-bandwidth collaboration (design sprints, retros, onboarding) on in-office days, so coming in has real payoff rather than a roll-call. (3) Feed data up honestly — if remote high-performers are being systematically undervalued, quantify it as a retention risk and report it up; that persuades decision-makers more than abstract fairness. You can't change the policy, but you can block its worst side effects.
Fully remote, hybrid, fully in-office — as a leader, should I have a preference? Or just follow the team?
No mode is absolutely better; fit is what matters. Bloom et al.'s hybrid experiment (2022, Nature) showed structured hybrid lost no productivity and significantly reduced attrition — but the prerequisites are async discipline, a clear written culture, and actively fighting proximity bias. So don't agonize over "which mode is best"; ask "what collaboration discipline fits this team and this kind of work": deep exploration favors async and quiet; high-collaboration early-stage projects need more sync. Your job is to design the rhythm of when-to-sync and when-to-async, not to pick a side in a three-way choice.
Do these principles carry cultural differences? In cultures that weigh hierarchy and "face time" more heavily, do async and remote trust actually work?
Yes, and you can't copy-paste. In high-context, high-hierarchy cultures, "being present in person" carries more trust and face, so pure async can read as distant or even disrespectful. The adaptation is blend, don't replace: first lay the trust foundation with a few high-quality synchronous touches (especially relationship-building, face-giving occasions), then run async work discipline on top of it. The principles hold (manage output, guard against bias, write clearly), but the rhythm and ritual of how they land must flex with culture — treat "async-first" as a direction, not a one-size-fits-all dogma.
